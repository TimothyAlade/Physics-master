Chapter 16: Electromagnetism

📙 SS3 First Term ⏱️ Reading: 35 mins

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Explain the magnetic effect of electric current
  • Describe electromagnets and their applications
  • State and apply Fleming's Left Hand Rule (motor effect)
  • Explain electromagnetic induction (Faraday's Law)
  • State Lenz's Law
  • Describe generators and transformers

16.1 Magnetic Effect of Electric Current

📌 Oersted's Discovery (1820): An electric current flowing through a wire produces a magnetic field around it. This is the principle behind electromagnetism.
  • Straight wire: Magnetic field forms concentric circles around the wire
  • Right-Hand Grip Rule: Thumb = current direction, fingers = magnetic field direction
  • Solenoid (coil): Produces a magnetic field similar to a bar magnet

16.2 Electromagnets

📌 Electromagnet: A temporary magnet produced by passing electric current through a coil (solenoid) wound around a soft iron core.

Factors affecting strength of electromagnet:

  • Number of turns in the coil (more turns = stronger)
  • Current flowing through coil (higher current = stronger)
  • Nature of core material (soft iron is best)

Applications: Electric bells, relays, loudspeakers, MRI machines, magnetic cranes (lifting scrap metal).

16.3 Force on a Current-Carrying Conductor (Motor Effect)

📌 Motor Effect: A current-carrying conductor placed in a magnetic field experiences a force. This is the principle behind electric motors.
F = BIL sin θ

Where: F = force (N), B = magnetic flux density (Tesla, T), I = current (A), L = length of conductor (m), θ = angle between current and field

Fleming's Left Hand Rule (Motor Rule)

✋ Fleming's Left Hand Rule:
• ThuMb = Motion (force direction)
• First Finger = Field (North to South)
• SeCond Finger = Current (+ to −)
📐 Example: A wire of length 0.5 m carries 3 A at 90° to a magnetic field of 0.4 T. Find force.
F = 0.4 × 3 × 0.5 × sin(90°) = 0.6 N

16.4 Electromagnetic Induction

📌 Electromagnetic Induction: The production of an EMF (voltage) in a conductor when it is exposed to a changing magnetic field. Discovered by Michael Faraday in 1831.

Faraday's Law

ε = −N (ΔΦ / Δt)

Where: ε = induced EMF (V), N = number of turns, Φ = magnetic flux (Weber, Wb), t = time (s)

The induced EMF is proportional to the rate of change of magnetic flux.

Lenz's Law

📌 Lenz's Law: The direction of the induced current is such that it opposes the change that produced it. This explains the negative sign in Faraday's Law.
  • Moving a magnet INTO a coil → induced current opposes the motion (repels)
  • Moving a magnet OUT of a coil → induced current opposes the motion (attracts)

16.6 Transformers

📌 Transformer: A device that changes the voltage of an alternating current (AC) using electromagnetic induction.
Vₚ/Vₛ = Nₚ/Nₛ = Iₛ/Iₚ
  • Step-Up Transformer: Nₛ > Nₚ → Vₛ > Vₚ (increases voltage)
  • Step-Down Transformer: Nₛ < Nₚ → Vₛ < Vₚ (decreases voltage)
📐 Example: A transformer has 500 primary turns and 50 secondary turns. If primary voltage is 240 V, find secondary voltage.
Vₛ = Vₚ × (Nₛ/Nₚ) = 240 × (50/500) = 24 V (step-down)

📝 Chapter 16 Summary

  • Current → Magnetic Field (Oersted, Right-Hand Grip Rule)
  • Electromagnet: Solenoid + soft iron core
  • Motor Effect: F = BIL (Fleming's Left Hand Rule)
  • Electromagnetic Induction: Changing magnetic field → EMF (Faraday's Law)
  • Lenz's Law: Induced current opposes change
  • Transformer: Vₚ/Vₛ = Nₚ/Nₛ
