Chapter 8: Newton's Laws of Motion

📗 SS2 First Term ⏱️ Reading: 40 mins

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • State and explain Newton's three laws of motion
  • Define inertia and its relationship to mass
  • Apply Newton's second law (F = ma) to solve problems
  • Explain action-reaction pairs
  • Solve problems involving connected bodies and tension
  • Apply Newton's laws to real-life situations

8.1 Introduction to Newton's Laws

Sir Isaac Newton (1642–1727) formulated three fundamental laws that govern the motion of objects. These laws form the foundation of classical mechanics.

💡 Newton's laws describe how forces affect motion. They apply to everyday objects from falling apples to orbiting planets.

8.2 Newton's First Law (Law of Inertia)

📌 Newton's First Law: An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion with the same speed and in the same direction, unless acted upon by an unbalanced (net) force.

Inertia

📌 Inertia: The tendency of an object to resist changes in its state of motion. The greater the mass, the greater the inertia.
📐 Examples of Newton's First Law:
• A book on a table stays at rest until someone pushes it.
• Passengers lurch forward when a bus suddenly stops (their bodies want to keep moving).
• A ball rolling on a rough surface eventually stops due to friction (unbalanced force).
• In space, a spacecraft keeps moving without fuel (no friction).
MassInertia
Small massSmall inertia – easy to start/stop
Large massLarge inertia – difficult to start/stop

8.3 Newton's Second Law (F = ma)

📌 Newton's Second Law: The acceleration of an object is directly proportional to the net force acting on it and inversely proportional to its mass.
F = m × a
Where: F = net force (N), m = mass (kg), a = acceleration (m/s²)

Key Relationships:

  • Greater force → Greater acceleration (directly proportional)
  • Greater mass → Smaller acceleration (inversely proportional)
  • 1 N = 1 kg × 1 m/s²
📐 Example 1: A 5 kg object experiences a net force of 20 N. Find its acceleration.
a = F/m = 20/5 = 4 m/s²
📐 Example 2: A car of mass 1000 kg accelerates at 3 m/s². Find the net force.
F = ma = 1000 × 3 = 3000 N
📐 Example 3: A force of 50 N gives an object an acceleration of 10 m/s². Find its mass.
m = F/a = 50/10 = 5 kg

8.4 Newton's Third Law (Action-Reaction)

📌 Newton's Third Law: For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction. Forces always occur in pairs.

Characteristics of Action-Reaction Pairs:

  • Equal in magnitude
  • Opposite in direction
  • Act on different objects (never cancel each other)
  • Same type of force (both gravitational, both contact, etc.)
📐 Examples of Newton's Third Law:
• Rocket propulsion: Rocket pushes gas backward → Gas pushes rocket forward
• Walking: Foot pushes ground backward → Ground pushes foot forward
• Swimming: Hands push water backward → Water pushes swimmer forward
• Jumping: Legs push ground down → Ground pushes person up
• Recoil of a gun: Bullet pushed forward → Gun pushed backward
⚠️ Common Misconception: Action and reaction forces do NOT cancel because they act on different objects. If they canceled, nothing would ever move!

8.5 Applications of Newton's Laws

Linear Momentum

📌 Momentum (p): The product of mass and velocity.
p = m × v
Unit: kg·m/s  |  Momentum is a vector quantity.
F = Δp / Δt   (Force = Rate of change of momentum)

Conservation of Momentum

In a closed system (no external forces), the total momentum before an interaction equals the total momentum after.
m₁u₁ + m₂u₂ = m₁v₁ + m₂v₂
📐 Example: A 2 kg ball moving at 5 m/s collides with a stationary 3 kg ball. After collision, the 2 kg ball moves at 1 m/s. Find the velocity of the 3 kg ball.

Before: (2 × 5) + (3 × 0) = 10 kg·m/s
After: (2 × 1) + (3 × v) = 2 + 3v
10 = 2 + 3v → 3v = 8 → v = 2.67 m/s

8.6 Impulse

📌 Impulse (J): The product of force and the time for which it acts. It equals the change in momentum.
J = F × Δt = Δp = m(v - u)
Unit: N·s (Newton-second)
📐 Example: A force of 100 N acts on a ball for 0.5 seconds. Find the impulse and the change in momentum.
J = 100 × 0.5 = 50 N·s (Change in momentum = 50 kg·m/s)

8.7 Tension in Connected Bodies

When objects are connected by a rope or string, the tension (T) is the same throughout the rope (assuming massless rope and frictionless pulley).

📐 Example: Two masses of 3 kg and 5 kg are connected over a frictionless pulley. Find the acceleration and tension.

Net force = (5 - 3) × 9.8 = 19.6 N
Total mass = 3 + 5 = 8 kg
a = F/m = 19.6/8 = 2.45 m/s²
T = 3 × (9.8 + 2.45) = 36.75 N

📝 Chapter 8 Summary

  • 1st Law (Inertia): Object stays at rest/motion unless net force acts
  • 2nd Law: F = ma (Force = mass × acceleration)
  • 3rd Law: Action = Reaction (equal and opposite, on different objects)
  • Momentum: p = mv (kg·m/s)
  • Impulse: J = FΔt = Δp (N·s)
  • Conservation of Momentum: Total momentum before = after (in closed system)
