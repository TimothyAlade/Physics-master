Chapter 11: Heat

📗 SS2 First Term ⏱️ Reading: 35 mins

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Distinguish between heat and temperature
  • Explain different types of thermometers
  • Describe thermal expansion of solids, liquids, and gases
  • State and apply the gas laws (Boyle's, Charles's, Pressure law)
  • Calculate heat energy using specific heat capacity
  • Explain latent heat and phase changes

11.1 Heat vs Temperature

📌 Heat: A form of energy that flows from a hotter body to a colder body. SI Unit: Joule (J).
📌 Temperature: A measure of the degree of hotness or coldness of a body. SI Unit: Kelvin (K).
HeatTemperature
Form of energy (Joules)Measure of hotness (Kelvin/°C)
Depends on massDoes not depend on mass
Flows from hot to coldDetermines direction of heat flow
Measured by calorimeterMeasured by thermometer

11.3 Thermal Expansion

📌 Thermal Expansion: Increase in size of a material when heated. Most substances expand when heated and contract when cooled.

Expansion of Solids

Linear Expansion: ΔL = αL₀ΔT
Area Expansion: ΔA = 2αA₀ΔT
Volume Expansion: ΔV = 3αV₀ΔT

Where: α = linear expansivity, L₀ = original length, ΔT = temperature change

Applications of Expansion

  • Gaps in railway lines — allow for expansion in hot weather
  • Bimetallic strips — used in thermostats, fire alarms
  • Expansion joints in bridges — prevent cracking

Expansion of Liquids

Liquids expand more than solids. Water has an anomalous expansion — it expands when cooled from 4°C to 0°C (ice floats).

Expansion of Gases

Gases expand the most when heated. This leads to the gas laws.

11.4 Gas Laws

Boyle's Law (Pressure-Volume)

📌 Boyle's Law: For a fixed mass of gas at constant temperature, pressure is inversely proportional to volume.
P₁V₁ = P₂V₂   (at constant T)
📐 Example: Gas at 200 kPa occupies 3 m³. If pressure increases to 600 kPa (constant T), find new volume.
V₂ = P₁V₁/P₂ = (200 × 3)/600 = 1 m³

Charles's Law (Volume-Temperature)

📌 Charles's Law: For a fixed mass of gas at constant pressure, volume is directly proportional to absolute temperature.
V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂   (at constant P)
📐 Example: Gas at 27°C (300 K) occupies 2 m³. At 327°C (600 K), what volume?
V₂ = V₁ × (T₂/T₁) = 2 × (600/300) = 4 m³

Pressure Law (Gay-Lussac's Law)

📌 Pressure Law: For a fixed mass of gas at constant volume, pressure is directly proportional to absolute temperature.
P₁/T₁ = P₂/T₂   (at constant V)

General Gas Law (Combined)

P₁V₁/T₁ = P₂V₂/T₂

11.5 Heat Capacity and Specific Heat Capacity

📌 Heat Capacity (C): Heat energy required to raise the temperature of a body by 1 K (or 1°C). Unit: J/K.
📌 Specific Heat Capacity (c): Heat required to raise temperature of 1 kg of a substance by 1 K. Unit: J/(kg·K).
Q = mcΔT

Where: Q = heat energy (J), m = mass (kg), c = specific heat capacity, ΔT = temperature change

📐 Example: How much heat to raise 2 kg of water (c = 4200 J/kg·K) from 20°C to 100°C?
Q = 2 × 4200 × 80 = 672,000 J (672 kJ)

11.6 Latent Heat

📌 Latent Heat: Heat energy absorbed or released during a change of state without a change in temperature.
Q = mL

Where: L = specific latent heat (J/kg)

  • Latent Heat of Fusion: Solid ↔ Liquid
  • Latent Heat of Vaporization: Liquid ↔ Gas

📝 Chapter 11 Summary

  • Heat is energy (J); Temperature is hotness (K/°C)
  • Thermal Expansion: Solids, liquids, and gases expand when heated
  • Boyle's Law: P₁V₁ = P₂V₂ (constant T)
  • Charles's Law: V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂ (constant P)
  • Heat Energy: Q = mcΔT
  • Latent Heat: Q = mL (phase change)
🎯 WAEC/NECO Tip: Gas law calculations (Boyle's, Charles's) and Q = mcΔT appear frequently! Always convert temperature to Kelvin for gas laws.
