Chapter 10: Pressure

📗 SS2 First Term ⏱️ Reading: 35 mins

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Define pressure and state its SI unit
  • Calculate pressure in solids
  • Explain pressure in liquids (hydrostatic pressure)
  • Describe atmospheric pressure
  • State and apply Pascal's principle
  • Explain applications of pressure in everyday life

10.1 What is Pressure?

📌 Pressure: Force acting perpendicularly per unit area.
SI Unit: Pascal (Pa)  |  1 Pa = 1 N/m²
P = F / A

Where: P = pressure (Pa or N/m²), F = force (N), A = area (m²)

📐 Example 1: A force of 500 N acts on an area of 2 m². Find the pressure.
P = 500 / 2 = 250 Pa
📐 Example 2: A woman of mass 60 kg stands on one heel of area 2 cm². Find pressure on the floor.
F = mg = 60 × 9.8 = 588 N
A = 2 cm² = 0.0002 m²
P = 588 / 0.0002 = 2,940,000 Pa (2.94 MPa)
💡 Key Relationship:
• Small area → High pressure (sharp knife cuts easily)
• Large area → Low pressure (snowshoes prevent sinking)
• Same force, different area → Different pressure

10.2 Pressure in Solids

In solids, pressure depends on the force applied and the area of contact.

ApplicationPrinciple
Sharp knifeSmall area → High pressure → Cuts easily
Blunt knifeLarge area → Low pressure → Hard to cut
Foundation of buildingsWide base → Low pressure → Stable
Tractor tiresWide tires → Low pressure → Doesn't sink in mud
Drawing pin/NailSharp point → High pressure → Penetrates easily

10.3 Pressure in Liquids

📌 Liquid Pressure: Pressure exerted by a liquid due to its weight. It acts equally in all directions at a given depth.
P = ρgh

Where: P = pressure (Pa), ρ = density of liquid (kg/m³), g = 9.8 m/s², h = height/depth (m)

📐 Example 1: Find pressure at depth 10 m in water (ρ = 1000 kg/m³).
P = 1000 × 9.8 × 10 = 98,000 Pa
📐 Example 2: A tank contains oil of density 800 kg/m³ to height 5 m. Find pressure at bottom.
P = 800 × 9.8 × 5 = 39,200 Pa
📊 Properties of Liquid Pressure:
• Increases with depth (P ∝ h)
• Increases with density (P ∝ ρ)
• Acts equally in all directions at same depth
• Independent of shape of container
• Same horizontal level → Same pressure

10.4 Atmospheric Pressure

📌 Atmospheric Pressure: Pressure exerted by the weight of air in the atmosphere. At sea level: 101,325 Pa (≈ 1.01 × 10⁵ Pa).

Standard atmospheric pressure = 760 mmHg = 76 cmHg = 1 atm = 101.3 kPa

Measuring Atmospheric Pressure

  • Mercury Barometer: Measures atmospheric pressure using mercury column
  • Aneroid Barometer: Uses flexible metal box (no liquid)
  • Manometer: Measures gas pressure relative to atmospheric pressure
🌍 Why doesn't atmospheric pressure crush us? Our bodies have internal pressure that balances the external atmospheric pressure. We're in equilibrium!

10.5 Pascal's Principle

📌 Pascal's Principle: Pressure applied to an enclosed fluid is transmitted equally and undiminished to all parts of the fluid and to the walls of the container.
P₁ = P₂   →   F₁/A₁ = F₂/A₂
📐 Hydraulic Press: A force of 100 N is applied to a small piston of area 0.01 m². If the large piston has area 0.5 m², what force can it lift?
F₂ = F₁ × (A₂/A₁) = 100 × (0.5/0.01) = 100 × 50 = 5000 N

Applications of Pascal's Principle

  • Hydraulic press/lift: Lifts heavy objects (cars in garage)
  • Hydraulic brakes: Car braking system
  • Hydraulic jack: Lifting vehicles to change tires
  • Syringe: Medical injections

10.6 Summary of Pressure Formulas

SituationFormula
Pressure in solidsP = F/A
Pressure in liquidsP = ρgh
Atmospheric pressure (sea level)1.013 × 10⁵ Pa
Pascal's PrincipleF₁/A₁ = F₂/A₂

📝 Chapter 10 Summary

  • Pressure: P = F/A (Pascals, Pa)
  • Liquid Pressure: P = ρgh (increases with depth)
  • Atmospheric Pressure: 1.013 × 10⁵ Pa at sea level
  • Pascal's Principle: Pressure transmits equally in enclosed fluid
  • Hydraulic systems: Force multiplication using different piston areas
