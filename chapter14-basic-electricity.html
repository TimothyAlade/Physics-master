Chapter 14: Basic Electricity

📗 SS2 Second Term ⏱️ Reading: 35 mins

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Define electric charge, current, voltage, and resistance
  • State Ohm's Law and apply it to solve problems
  • Distinguish between series and parallel circuits
  • Calculate effective resistance in circuits
  • Explain the difference between conductors and insulators

14.1 Electric Charge

📌 Electric Charge (Q): A fundamental property of matter that causes it to experience a force in an electromagnetic field. Two types: positive (+) and negative (−).
SI Unit: Coulomb (C)
⚡ Basic Rules:
• Like charges repel (+ and +, or − and −)
• Unlike charges attract (+ and −)
• Charge is conserved (cannot be created or destroyed)

14.2 Electric Current

📌 Electric Current (I): The rate of flow of electric charge. SI Unit: Ampere (A).
I = Q / t
Where: I = current (A), Q = charge (C), t = time (s)
📐 Example: 30 C of charge flows through a wire in 6 seconds. Find the current.
I = 30 / 6 = 5 A
  • Conventional current: Flows from positive (+) to negative (−)
  • Electron flow: Electrons move from negative (−) to positive (+)
  • Measured using an ammeter connected in series

14.3 Voltage (Potential Difference)

📌 Voltage (V): The electrical pressure that drives current through a circuit. Also called potential difference (p.d.). SI Unit: Volt (V).
V = W / Q   or   V = E / Q
Measured using a voltmeter connected in parallel.

14.4 Resistance and Ohm's Law

📌 Resistance (R): The opposition to the flow of electric current. SI Unit: Ohm (Ω).
📌 Ohm's Law: The current through a conductor is directly proportional to the voltage across it, provided temperature remains constant.
V = I × R
📐 Example 1: A resistor has 12 V across it and 3 A flowing through it. Find R.
R = V / I = 12 / 3 = 4 Ω
📐 Example 2: A 10 Ω resistor has 2 A flowing through it. Find V.
V = I × R = 2 × 10 = 20 V

14.5 Factors Affecting Resistance

R = ρL / A

Where: ρ = resistivity (Ω·m), L = length (m), A = cross-sectional area (m²)

  • Longer wire → More resistance (R ∝ L)
  • Thicker wire → Less resistance (R ∝ 1/A)
  • Higher temperature → More resistance (for metals)
  • Different materials → Different resistivity

14.6 Conductors and Insulators

ConductorsInsulators
Allow current to flow easilyResist current flow
Low resistanceHigh resistance
Examples: Copper, Silver, Aluminum, GoldRubber, Glass, Plastic, Wood, Air
Uses: Wires, cables, circuitsUses: Wire coating, safety gloves

14.7 Series and Parallel Circuits

Series Circuit

  • Same current flows through all components (I = I₁ = I₂)
  • Voltage divides across components (V = V₁ + V₂)
  • Rtotal = R₁ + R₂ + R₃ + ...

Parallel Circuit

  • Same voltage across all branches (V = V₁ = V₂)
  • Current divides through branches (I = I₁ + I₂)
  • 1/Rtotal = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + 1/R₃ + ...
📐 Series Example: 4 Ω and 6 Ω in series. Find total R.
R = 4 + 6 = 10 Ω
📐 Parallel Example: 3 Ω and 6 Ω in parallel. Find total R.
1/R = 1/3 + 1/6 = 3/6 = 1/2 → R = 2 Ω

📝 Chapter 14 Summary

  • Charge (Q): Coulombs (C)
  • Current (I): I = Q/t (Amperes, A)
  • Voltage (V): Electrical pressure (Volts, V)
  • Ohm's Law: V = IR
  • Resistance: R = ρL/A (Ohms, Ω)
  • Series: R = R₁ + R₂ | Parallel: 1/R = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂
🎯 WAEC/NECO Tip: Ohm's Law (V=IR) and series/parallel resistance calculations appear in almost every exam!
